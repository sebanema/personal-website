The Quest for Cosmic Justice in South Africa

February 13, 2026

Undressing Justice and Race-Based Laws in South Africa

Race-based laws in South Africa are a highly contentious and sensitive issue.

Most of the headlines you will see on the issue of race laws will be highly polarising, such as a Daily Maverick article titled: "It's grossly dishonest to claim South Africa has more race laws than ever before" or something like "Elon Musk slates South Africa's 'anti-white' race laws". Oversimplified headlines and video clips taken out of context are the primary weapons of public discourse; they allow people to strengthen their own narratives without ever having to engage with the counterargument.

To me, the real argument surrounding the prevailing issue of race-based laws is an unsensationalist one. It is not about proving they are as oppressive as apartheid race laws, nor about proving whether they are morally justified. Rather, it is about whether the vision of justice beneath these laws produces the outcomes it promises.

Dan Corder is a good example of someone who uses oversimplified headlines to strengthen his own narrative. In a video titled "The 142 modern race laws that are just like apartheid debunked", in a morally superior tone, Dan takes the perspective that very few people actually looked into race-laws in South Africa—and that in fact he actually did. He then proceeds to hype the sensationalist narrative of the 142 modern race laws that are just like a modern-day version of apartheid. He proceeds to "debunk" it by correctly highlighting these laws are provisions for previously-disadvantaged communities to address past injustices. He then goes on to swiftly say that this is the way "Just laws work"—again, in a morally superior tone. At this point he begins a strawman argument. Sure, he may rightfully argue that these laws are not as overtly racially oppressive as the laws that existed during apartheid. But by arguing against this point, he misleads his audience to think that anyone opposing these laws is racist. This is simply not the case. Of course it could be he was intentional in conducting a strawman argument, to curry favour of his eNCA viewers and producers to keep his show on the air.

For context, the Index of Race Law is an initiative by the South African Institute of Race Relations, which published an index on race-based laws. It tracks racially-based laws over time in South Africa, where over 324 racial acts of parliament have been adopted since 1910. 122 novel standalone racial acts of parliament have been adopted since 1994, and 145 racial acts of parliament are still operative today.

To assess whether race laws fulfil their underlying visions, one must examine their outcomes and how they operate in reality—in the courts.

South African Police Service v Solidarity obo Barnard (2014)

To illustrate the impact of race-based laws impacting individuals in the South African courts, one can look at the 2014 case of South African Police Service (SAPS) v Solidarity o.b.o. Barnard, which came to my attention through a report on racialised legislation in South Africa. In this instance, Captain Barnard, a member of the SAPS, sought a promotion to a superintendent vacancy. Despite achieving the top score during the evaluation process and receiving the selection committee's endorsement, her appointment was blocked. The National Commissioner of Police refused to promote her, citing the fact that her appointment would not advance "representivity" within the force. Even after the SAPS re-advertised the role and Captain Barnard once again proved to be the most qualified candidate, she was rejected a second time. Ultimately, the position was left entirely vacant.

During the legal proceedings, the court commented extensively on the complexity of Captain Barnard's identity, noting she belonged to a group categorised as "advantaged" (white persons) while also being part of a group that was historically marginalised (women). The court further emphasised the collective history of disadvantage faced by the African population and the necessity of measures to address that shared harm. In the final result, Captain Barnard lost her legal battle, as the Constitutional Court upheld the National Commissioner's choice to deny her the promotion.

The consequence of this ruling was that the post remained vacant, despite the fact that two African applicants were also recommended by the committee—who secured lower marks than Captain Barnard. In its reasoning, the court did not focus on whether the discrimination against Captain Barnard actually served to mitigate past racial wrongs; and instead, questioned if the discrimination was legally permissible under the mandate of representivity. The court concluded that leaving a post vacant rather than appointing a white individual was a superior outcome because it supposedly advanced equality. But how did this improve equality?

How the Constitution Defines Equality

To gain context of how the Constitutional Court was able to uphold the decision not to promote Captain Barnard on the basis of the colour of her skin, you need to understand South Africa's constitutional architecture, and the country it was designed to rebuild.

When apartheid officially ended in 1994, South Africa moved toward a new constitutional order, culminating in the 1996 Constitution. It is widely regarded in comparative constitutional scholarship as one of the most progressive in the world. It promised a grand vision: to heal the divisions of the past and build a new society founded on dignity, equality, and freedom.

In practice, equality is not a self-defining concept; meaning it rather depends on how a constitution defines and applies it. Many constitutions and international instruments adopt what is often called a formal equality model—equality before the law combined with a prohibition on discrimination in clear, universal terms. Botswana's Constitution provides a strong example of this approach. Section 15 states:

"No law shall make any provision that is discriminatory either of itself or in its effect."

International human rights instruments, such as the United Nations Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR) of 1948, affirm equality before the law. Article 7 of the UDHR states:

"All are equal before the law and are entitled without any discrimination to equal protection of the law."

Some countries, such as South Africa, Zimbabwe and India, describe equality in a different manner. Their constitutions do not frame equality solely as identical treatment before the law, but explicitly allow measures designed to advance groups historically disadvantaged by discrimination. These provisions are usually rooted in histories of colonialism, imperial rule, or racial hierarchy. Section 9(2) of South Africa's constitution reads:

"Equality includes the full and equal enjoyment of all rights and freedoms. To promote the achievement of equality, legislative and other measures designed to protect or advance persons, or categories of persons, disadvantaged by unfair discrimination may be taken".

In simple terms, the South African constitution does not define equality as identical treatment in all circumstances. It explicitly allows race-conscious measures if they are designed to advance those disadvantaged by past discrimination. Under the Constitutional Court's interpretation, such measures are not treated as unconstitutional discrimination, but as part of the equality project itself.

This point is crucial. When applied in reality, such as in Captain Barnard's case, the Court upheld the decision not to promote her because her appointment would not enhance racial representivity. The Constitution provided sufficient authority for the state to prioritise representivity over individual merit in that instance.

Captain Barnard was discriminated against because of the colour of her skin. Whether or not we support the promotion of equality of previously disadvantaged groups, it is impossible to justify that Captain Barnard was not affected by a constitutional law that applied its decree based on race.

The 3 Tiers of Racialised Legislation

With the premise that the Constitution, as applied in practice, has led to race-based discrimination in South Africa, it becomes necessary to distinguish between the different categories of race-based laws and the purposes they serve. Afriforum created a report on racialised legislation in South Africa which breaks down the racialised legislation into three buckets:

  • Anti-racism protections: These laws prohibit overt racial discrimination, such as preventing a person from being refused a rental lease based on their race. These laws are uncontroversial as they align with international human rights standards.
  • Explicit affirmative action laws ("equity laws"): This category includes expressly racial laws like the Employment Equity Act (EEA) and the B-BBEE Act, which aim to correct past discrimination by providing preferential treatment and setting numerical targets for designated groups.
  • "Representivity" rules: These provisions require that the staff, boards, or license holders of various institutions proportionally reflect national demographics, often preferring candidates solely based on belonging to the majority population group rather than proving they personally suffered from past discrimination. This was the type of race-based law that was applied in Captain Barnard's case.

Given apartheid's history, it seems rational that the post-1994 dispensation embraced race-conscious redress. In the apartheid South Africa regime, a few racial laws that overtly oppressed non-white population groups was not limited to, but included a few of the following:

  • Group Areas Act (1950): Enforced racially segregated residential and business zones, leading to large-scale forced removals of non-white communities from areas classified for white occupation.
  • Bantu Education Act (1953): Centralised control of Black education under the state and structured the curriculum to limit Black students to subordinate economic roles.
  • Separate Amenities Act (1953): Legalised racially segregated public facilities and explicitly permitted those facilities to be unequal in quality and provision.

There is no question that apartheid-era laws systematically disadvantaged entire population groups while they were in effect. The debate over today's race-based laws rests on a simpler claim: they exist to promote equality and transformation for previously disadvantaged individuals. And this goal itself justifies the costs involved. If the desired outcome of transformation is achieved, then any resulting discrimination against minority groups is, as Dan "the man" Corder puts it, considered "just."

Traditional vs Cosmic Justice

The word "just" carries significant baggage, particularly in a country shaped by South Africa's history. To understand why the race-based transformation laws emerged as they did, we must examine the vision of justice that shaped the ANC's constitutional project. In The Quest for Cosmic Justice, Thomas Sowell distinguishes between two fundamentally different conflicting ideas of justice: Traditional Justice and Cosmic Justice.

It is helpful to see the difference between these two ideas side-by-side:

Traditional justice is defined as a characteristic of a process rather than a specific result, concerned with equal treatment under the same rules. Within this framework, a situation is considered just if it is conducted under fair rules and with impartial participants, such as an unbiased judge and jury in a legal trial. The key characteristics that underpin traditional justice are the following:

  • Level Playing Field: This means that everyone plays by the same rules and is judged by the same standards. Take a game of rugby for example: both sides are judged by the same set of rules by the (hopefully unbiased) referee. If the game is judged by the standards of the rulebook, the game's outcome will be as just, regardless of whether it results in a "draw or a one-sided beating". Each side had their chance to change the outcome based on equal footing when the game began.
  • Human Scale of Knowledge: To apply traditional justice, one only needs to apply the same rules to everyone; it requires no prior knowledge of an individual's childhood, cultural heritage, or the complex historical influences that affected their ancestors.
  • Individual Focus: It deals with the interactions of flesh-and-blood human beings in the present.

Cosmic Justice, often referred to as "Social Justice", is a fundamentally different concept to Traditional Justice. It is a process concerned with the redress of outcomes, rather than the fairness of the process. It is concerned with reshaping outcomes to produce what is seen as fairer prospects between groups, seeking to eliminate undeserved disadvantages for selected groups. Rather than focusing on the rules of the game, it aims to put segments of society in the position they would have been in "but for some undeserved misfortune". Because cosmic justice is moralised, it becomes a powerful political idea. The key characteristics that underpin cosmic justice are the following:

  • Redressing "Undeserved Inequalities": It seeks to correct not only biased acts by individuals but also unmerited disadvantages arising from any source, including biological differences, geographical settings, or historical legacies.
  • The Requirement of Omniscience: Cosmic justice requires or assumes vastly more knowledge than traditional justice. It presumes to account for every variable and historical interaction that has shaped an individual or group over centuries—knowledge Sowell argues human beings do not possess.
  • "Anti-Social" Justice: Sowell argues that in its pursuit of results for a specific segment of society, cosmic justice often operates in disregard of the costs to other individuals or the requirements of society as a whole.
  • Group Abstractions: Unlike traditional justice, cosmic justice often deals with group abstractions extending over generations or even centuries, such as seeking reparations for "historic wrongs of the seventeenth century".

The central conflict between the two forms of justice is that of cost. Sowell argues that "justice at all costs" is not justice, because what is an injustice but the "arbitrary imposition of a cost on an innocent person?". While traditional justice weighs the practical costs of making specific changes, cosmic justice often ignores these costs for the sake of "symbolic expiation".

The Vision of Equality

A vital part of Sowell's thesis is the limitation of human agency. He argues that proponents of cosmic justice act as if they are God at the dawn of creation, able to say "Let there be equality". He contends we must instead begin with the universe we were born into and weigh the costs of making specific changes to achieve a specific end.

This counters directly against the "blank slate" assumption in South African policy, which suggests social engineering can perfectly mirror a hypothetical world where apartheid never happened. In the ANC's pursuit of transformation, the government does not want to ask the question "At what cost?"

Sowell spends significant time on the philosophy of John Rawls, who argues that "undeserved inequalities call for redress". He distinguishes between the two concepts of equality:

  • Formal Equality: Everyone plays by the same rules (Traditional Justice).
  • Fair Equality: Everyone has "equal prospects of success" (Cosmic Justice).

Sowell's critique is that to achieve "fair" equality, you must deliberately set aside equal rules and standards, which inherently creates a new form of injustice for those whose merits are bypassed. Although it is a noble goal to strive for equality, there has never been a society that has successfully achieved total equality. Furthermore, the quest for ultimate equality leads to communism, which is obviously a terrible outcome for the individuals within that society.

In South Africa, the quest for Cosmic Justice is ingrained in our policy architecture. Affirmative action compliance policies are implemented to redress what philosopher John Rawls calls "undeserved inequalities", most notably through:

  • Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment (B-BBEE): a scorecard-based system that shapes procurement, supplier decisions, and eligibility for many opportunities, with compliance burdens and penalties for misrepresentation (including fines up to 10% of annual turnover and possible imprisonment). In the mining industry, minimum black ownership thresholds of 26% and later 30% have been prescribed as a condition for operating rights.
  • The Employment Equity Act (EEA): a framework that requires designated employers (50+ employees) to implement race-based equity plans and to report against race-based targets, with access to many state contracts tied to compliance certification (Section 53).

This moral rationale was famously articulated by President Lyndon B. Johnson in his 1965 commencement address at Howard University:

"You do not take a man who, for years, has been hobbled by chains, liberate him, and bring him to the starting line of a race, saying, 'You are free to compete with all others', and still justly believe you have been completely fair".

The starting line logic is rhetorically powerful, because it makes unequal treatment feel like justice. However, Sowell argues that putting someone ahead through preferential treatment often fails to benefit the individual and destroys the very progress it seeks to promote. Instead of fostering advancement, legal entitlements create a "sense of entitlement" and a "lack of urgency" in beneficiaries regarding their own academic or professional preparation. For example, Sowell cites studies showing that minority students often feel less pressure to prepare themselves because they believe standards will simply be set aside for them.

In the quest for cosmic justice, the practical elements are often never addressed. Once the idea takes hold that equality requires race-conscious intervention, it becomes an extremely effective tool for gaining and sustaining political power—under the banner of "transformation." It then moves from a moral principle into institutional practice. And once it's embedded in institutions and enforced at scale, does it achieve what it intends to do?

Well, South Africa is a very good example of it playing out in reality.

What Cosmic Justice Produces in Practice

Compliance creating Economic Stagnation

Affirmative action policies, according to a study by the Free Market Foundation and the Solidarity Research Institute, are estimated to have cost the South African economy R5 trillion and resulted in the loss of 4 million jobs. The underlying point is that when firms must optimise for compliance as a condition of opportunity, resources shift away from growth, productivity, and hiring.

The incentive structure also changes acutely when non-compliance carries punitive risk. Aggressive equity laws, such as those allowing the Department of Employment and Labour to impose fines of between R1.5 million and 10% of an employer's annual turnover, have forced companies like Dis-Chem to impose moratoriums on promoting white individuals to avoid financial ruin. In the case of Dis-Chem, they openly admitted in an internal memorandum that, as a listed company, they could not afford to run the risk of a potential fine of 10% of their annual turnover for non-compliance with the government's racial equity mandates.

The Enrichment of the Black Elite

The quest for cosmic justice relies on group abstractions rather than flesh-and-blood individuals. Under representivity logic, appointing someone from a racial category is treated as compensation for what happened to that category, even if the individual themselves never personally suffered prejudice. Someone's specific experience is irrelevant. This is what Sowell calls "inter-temporal group abstractions," and creates what he calls symbolic expiation. In South Africa, that can mean the child of a tendepreneur, or black doctor, may receive preference over the child of a white factory worker, purely because the categories matter more than the people inside them.

The Rise of Intra-Racial Inequality

Since 1994, wealth inequality is no longer primarily inter-racial, but has become intra-racial. By 1996, the wealthiest 10th of the African population earned 250 times more than their lower-class counterparts. This same wealthy 10th of the African population accounted for a sixth of the total national income across all races. While the income gap between different racial groups has narrowed slightly, the income difference between a poor black person and a rich black person is widening. Equity laws like B-BBEE are often over-inclusive because they reward people who already cleared key barriers, while doing little for the poorest sectors of society.

Corruption and State Capture

The transformation project in South Africa, led by the ruling ANC party, has produced a mafia state. In placing "transformation" at the centre of state decision-making, the government created powerful discretionary authority over contracts, procurement, and appointments. That discretion, combined with race-based compliance frameworks, opened the door to systemic abuse. This abuse has became known as "state capture".

The Zondo Commission found that the ideals of empowerment were "grossly manipulated and abused to advance the interests of a few individuals". Instead of empowerment uplifting the disadvantaged majority, politically connected elites used empowerment structures to extract funds from the state.

Recent research suggests that R1 trillion has moved between just 100 politically connected individuals after decades of race-based policies like BEE. Instead of pursuing tenders that aligned with a value-for-money approach, officials contracted their personal shell-companies at exhorbitant costs that siphon state funds—all under the ruse of transformation.

All these reasons explain why so many state-run institutions—like Eskom, Transnet and SAA just to list a few—have failed so abysmally. A strong personal account of the internal collapse of Eskom is shared by former CEO Andre de Ruyter in his book "Truth to Power".

Anti-Social Costs Paid by the Poor

Probably the most tragic of them all, is the cost to the impoverished majority group of black people. While a small black elite prospers, the broader South African society, particularly the poor, bears the anti-social costs of these policies. We have among the worst unemployment rates in the world, with an expanded measure of unemployment at 42.9% in Q2 2025.

Despite the over 50 laws mandating representivity; poverty and disparity still exist and are worsening. In many cases, actual qualifications and skills are treated as obstacles because they interfere with the cosmic goal of representivity.

A moral project claiming to uplift the worst-off should be judged by whether the worst-off are actually better off. If, after thirty years, the poorest remain trapped while the only tangible result is the enrichment of a small black elite alongside a roster of entrenched oligarchs, then the vision itself is a failure.

The Cost of Cosmic Justice

South Africa's transformation project and race-based laws rest on a quest for Cosmic Justice. This is the idea that promoting equality requires race-conscious intervention aimed at correcting past injustices. Captain Barnard's story was one of many cases where individuals have suffered under race-based laws that still shape people's lives today. In South Africa's current dispensation, representivity is treated as a higher good than individual merit—even to the point of leaving a post vacant.

The quest for cosmic justice is a morally compelling vision, especially in a country shaped by historical racial oppression. It captured the minds of the ANC leaders and the people they represented as a "just" reaction to a past of racial oppression. But more than thirty years after the end of apartheid, South Africa remains far from the ideals promised in the ANC's Freedom Charter. Voters are also beginning to realise this; recognising the ANC cannot continue using apartheid as a scapegoat for its present-day incompetence.

The crux of Sowell's teaching reminds me of the old proverb:

The road to hell is paved with good intentions.

Every solution is only as useful as the costs it is willing to confront. In South Africa's case, it is currently paying a hefty price for its ideals, and its people ultimately foot the bill.
